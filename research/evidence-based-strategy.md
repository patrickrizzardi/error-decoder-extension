## 2. Candidate Evaluation: 3 Paths Compared {#2-candidate-evaluation}

### Path A: AI Estimating Tool for Contractors (Trades)

**The Bull Case:**
- $2.1 TRILLION market, 30% admin overhead waste (SignalFire)
- AI in construction: $3.93B (2024) → $22.68B by 2032, 26.38% CAGR (Fortune Business Insights, SNS Insider)
- By 2027, 60%+ of US construction firms expected to integrate AI cost analysis (RICS survey)
- Contractors save 50-80% on estimating time with AI tools (Beam AI, Togal.AI)
- 62% of small plumbing businesses cite high cost as primary tech adoption barrier (Jobber 2025) — price sensitivity = opportunity for affordable tool
- ServiceTitan charges $500/tech/month — small shops can't afford it

**The Bear Case (Why I DON'T Recommend This):**
- **Well-funded competitors already here**: Handoff AI ($25.45M raised, Y Combinator, Nemetschek, Greycroft) has 10,000+ contractors. XBuild ($19M Series A, backed by Andreessen Horowitz). These aren't small players.
- **Domain expertise gap**: Construction pricing, local costs, permitting codes, labor rates — this requires deep industry knowledge Patrick doesn't have. Handoff's AI is "trained on real residential construction projects, real contractor pricing, and real job site conditions." You can't fake this with a wrapper.
- **Customer acquisition is hard**: Contractors aren't on Reddit, Product Hunt, or Hacker News. They're on job sites. Marketing to them requires trade shows, local SEO, Facebook groups, and word-of-mouth — a different playbook than dev tools.
- **User complaints about existing tools show the problem**: "Estimates come back extremely high" and "doesn't understand labor costs or permitting for their state" — meaning getting the AI RIGHT is the entire product. It's not a UI problem, it's a data/accuracy problem.
- **78% of entry-level plumbers use digital tools but 41% of veterans are skeptical** (Jobber 2025) — adoption barrier with key decision-makers.

**Verdict**: Massive market but the competition has $44M+ in funding and years of construction-specific training data. Solo dev entering this space in 2026 is bringing a knife to a gunfight. Not impossible but not the FASTEST path.

### Path B: Chrome Extension (Developer-Focused)

**The Bull Case:**
- Proven solo dev revenue model: Closet Tools ($42K/mo), GMass ($130-200K/mo), CSS Scan ($100K+ total), Easy Folders ($3.7K/mo) — all built by small teams or solo devs (ExtensionPay, Starter Story)
- 70-85% profit margins (ExtensionPay)
- Built-in distribution via Chrome Web Store (180M+ Chrome users)
- Low development time: can ship MVP in days, not months
- Patrick's TypeScript skills directly applicable
- Freemium model proven: free tier builds reviews/installs, paid tier generates revenue
- AI extension market growing at 22.5% CAGR (Extension Radar)

**The Bear Case:**
- The "average" is misleading — most extensions make nothing. The $862K/year figure is for SUCCESSFUL ones. Median is much lower.
- Discovery in Chrome Web Store is getting harder as it gets crowded
- Need to find a specific niche that isn't already solved
- Revenue ceiling may be lower than full SaaS (typical indie dev extensions: $3-10K/mo)

**Key Success Pattern** (from case studies):
- Solve ONE specific pain point extremely well
- Free tier that's genuinely useful (drives installs and reviews)
- Paid tier at $5-20/mo for power features
- Content marketing / SEO for your niche keywords

**Verdict**: Lowest risk, fastest to ship, proven model, plays to Patrick's exact skills. Revenue ceiling is moderate but $3-10K/mo is realistic within 6-12 months with good execution. That's $36-120K/year EXTRA toward debt.

### Path C: AI Micro-SaaS (Web App)

**The Bull Case:**
- Higher revenue ceiling than extensions ($10K-100K+ MRR possible)
- Full control over the experience (not constrained by browser extension APIs)
- Can charge more ($19-49/mo vs $5-15/mo for extensions)
- Solo dev success stories: Chatbase ($5M ARR from solo founder), Photo AI ($132K MRR), FormulAI ($47K/mo), Taplio ($150K/mo with 80%+ margins)

**The Bear Case:**
- Takes longer to build (weeks-months vs days for extension)
- Marketing is harder (no built-in distribution like Chrome Web Store)
- Need to drive ALL your own traffic (SEO, content, social)
- 70% make under $1K/mo — the failure rate is real
- Higher infrastructure costs (hosting, API calls, database)

**The Winning Pattern** (from Senja.io case study and others):
1. Build MVP in 1-2 weeks
2. Launch on Product Hunt, Reddit, Indie Hackers
3. Content marketing / SEO for long-tail keywords (compounds over months)
4. Charge from day one — even $9/mo validates demand
5. 10-20 direct outreach messages per week to potential users
6. Median 24 months to $1M ARR but first $1K MRR in 2-6 months

**Verdict**: Highest ceiling but longest path to first dollar. Best if Patrick finds a niche where he has genuine domain expertise AND the audience is reachable online.

## 3. The Recommendation & Why {#3-recommendation}

### Recommended: Hybrid Approach — Chrome Extension FIRST, Then Expand to Full SaaS

**Why this order matters:**

1. **Chrome extension is the fastest path to revenue.** Ship in days, not months. Chrome Web Store provides discovery. Patrick needs MOMENTUM — his pattern is overscoping and abandoning. Getting a first paying customer in weeks, not months, changes psychology.

2. **Extension validates the market BEFORE you build bigger.** If 1,000 people install it and 50 pay $9/mo, you've got $450 MRR and PROOF. Then build the full SaaS version.

3. **Extensions are marketing for the SaaS.** Free extension users become your email list, your beta testers, your first SaaS customers.

4. **Patrick's skills are perfectly matched.** TypeScript, Node.js, SQL — this is Chrome extension tech stack exactly. No new skills needed.

### But WHAT Extension?

Based on the research, the SQL/database tool space is saturated for basic text-to-SQL (12+ competitors). BUT there are specific gaps:

**Top 3 Extension Ideas (Ranked by Evidence):**

#### Idea 1: AI Database Schema Explorer / Documentation Generator
- **What**: Chrome extension that adds AI to phpMyAdmin, pgAdmin, DBeaver web UI, or any database admin tool. Hover over a table/column and get instant AI-powered documentation, relationship mapping, and optimization suggestions.
- **Gap**: No existing tool does this as a lightweight overlay. Wren AI costs $99-299/mo. DBHub is developer-focused CLI tool. Nothing sits on top of existing DB interfaces.
- **Audience**: Every developer who's inherited a database with no documentation (basically everyone)
- **Why it could work**: Solves a universal developer pain point ("WTF does this column actually do?"), works with tools they already use, low barrier to install

#### Idea 2: AI Code Review Assistant (GitHub/GitLab Overlay)
- **What**: Chrome extension that adds AI-powered code review suggestions directly in GitHub/GitLab PR views. Not a full CI bot — just instant inline suggestions as you review.
- **Gap**: CodeRabbit and others are full CI integrations ($12-24/mo). No lightweight extension-only approach.
- **Audience**: Every developer reviewing PRs
- **Caution**: This space is getting competitive. Would need a very specific angle.

#### Idea 3: AI Error Decoder / Stack Trace Explainer
- **What**: Chrome extension that detects error messages and stack traces on any webpage (Stack Overflow, GitHub Issues, logs) and provides instant AI-powered explanations and fixes.
- **Gap**: People paste errors into ChatGPT manually. No extension auto-detects and explains them in-context.
- **Audience**: Every developer who googles error messages (all of them)
- **Why it could work**: Massive frequency of use, low-effort value prop, easy to demonstrate

### My Actual Pick: Idea 3 — AI Error Decoder

**Why:**
- **Highest frequency of use** — developers hit errors CONSTANTLY. Multiple times per day.
- **Easiest to demo and explain** — "It reads your error and tells you what's wrong. Install it."
- **Broadest audience** — works for every language, every framework, every skill level
- **Free tier is genuinely useful** — 5 decodes/day free, unlimited for $9/mo
- **Content marketing gold** — every "how to fix [error]" blog post is a potential customer
- **Low API cost per use** — single error decode is ~500 tokens, pennies per use
- **Upsell to SaaS** — full web app with history, team sharing, error pattern analysis

## 4. Full Build Plan: Start to Finish {#4-build-plan}

**What we're building:**

## 5. Marketing & Sales Plan for a First-Timer {#5-marketing-plan}

### You Know Nothing About Marketing — That's Fine. Here's the Playbook.

**Rule 1: Pick 2 channels. Master them. Ignore everything else.**
(Source: NxCode SaaS Marketing Playbook 2026, Solo Founder Marketing)

Your 2 channels:
1. **SEO / Content Marketing** — Write articles targeting developer error keywords
2. **Reddit / Developer Communities** — Be helpful, not salesy

**Rule 2: Your marketing budget is $0. Your budget is TIME.**
(Source: Multiple zero-budget SaaS case studies)

| Activity | Time/Week | What You Do |
|----------|-----------|-------------|
| Write 2 blog posts | 2-3 hours | I draft them, you review and publish |
| Reddit engagement | 1-2 hours | Answer questions, share insights (NOT pitching) |
| Respond to user feedback | 1 hour | Chrome Web Store reviews, email, GitHub issues |
| Weekly metrics check | 30 min | Installs, conversions, MRR |
| **Total** | **5-7 hours/week** | |

**Rule 3: Your first 100 customers come from being helpful, not from ads.**

The proven first-100-customers playbook (Source: Indie Hackers case study of 7 founders):

1. **Users 1-10**: Friends, colleagues, Reddit posts, Show HN
2. **Users 10-50**: Content marketing starts compounding, Chrome Web Store organic discovery
3. **Users 50-100**: Word of mouth from satisfied users, SEO traffic building
4. **Users 100+**: SEO is now a machine. Content you wrote months ago keeps bringing people in.

**Timeline**: Most founders report 6-8 weeks from community engagement to first paying customer. Some as fast as 3-4 weeks. (Source: Indie Hackers GTM case studies)

### What "Marketing" Actually Looks Like Day-to-Day

**Week 1-2**: Launch posts. Reddit, HN, Indie Hackers, Twitter/X. Be transparent: "Solo dev, first product, here's what it does, would love feedback."

**Week 3-8**: Content grind. 2 articles/week. Each one targets a specific error message that developers Google. Examples:
- "TypeError: Cannot read properties of undefined — What It Means and How to Fix It"
- "CORS errors explained: Why your API call is blocked"
- "MySQL error 1045: Access denied — Complete troubleshooting guide"

Each article naturally mentions: "Want instant error decoding? Try [Extension Name]."

**Week 8+**: Evaluate what's working. Double down on top-performing content. Start building email list. Consider ProductHunt launch (they allow re-launches with major updates).

### Pricing Strategy

**Freemium with hard limit:**
- **Free**: 5 error decodes per day (enough to be useful, not enough for power users)
- **Pro**: $9/month or $79/year (unlimited decodes + history + priority models)

**Why $9/mo**:
- Below the "need to think about it" threshold for developers
- Developer tool freemium-to-paid conversion averages 11.7% (Source: First Page Sage 2026)
- At 11.7% conversion: 1,000 free users = ~117 paid = $1,053 MRR
- Compare to general SaaS conversion of 2.6% — dev tools convert WAY better
